Ocean Acidification

Ocean acidification, often called "the other CO₂ problem," is a direct consequence of the oceans absorbing nearly 30% of human-caused carbon emissions. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it triggers chemical reactions that increase acidity, reducing the water's pH and depleting carbonate ions—essential building blocks for marine life. Since the pre-industrial era, ocean acidity has risen by 30%, posing severe risks to marine ecosystems.

Shell-forming organisms like corals, oysters, and plankton struggle to survive as acidic waters dissolve their calcium carbonate structures. Coral reefs, which support 25% of marine species, face erosion and bleaching, jeopardizing biodiversity and coastal protection. Meanwhile, tiny pteropods (key food sources for fish) experience shell degradation, disrupting entire food chains. Fisheries and aquaculture industries, worth billions, are at risk as shellfish populations decline.

The impacts extend beyond ecology—coastal economies, food security, and carbon storage are threatened. While reducing CO₂ emissions is the ultimate solution, local measures like protecting mangroves, reducing pollution, and restoring seagrass beds can help mitigate damage. Without urgent global action, ocean acidification will intensify, causing irreversible harm to marine life and the millions who depend on healthy oceans for survival. The time to act is now—before the seas become too hostile for the life they sustain.
