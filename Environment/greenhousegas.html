Greenhouse Gas Emissions (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O)

Greenhouse gases (GHGs) such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O) trap heat in the atmosphere, driving global warming and climate change. CO₂, primarily from fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and industrial processes, is the most significant contributor, accounting for about 76% of global emissions. CH₄, though less abundant, is 25 times more potent than CO₂ over a century and stems from agriculture (livestock and rice paddies), landfills, and fossil fuel extraction. N₂O, mainly from agricultural fertilizers and industrial activities, has nearly 300 times the warming potential of CO₂ and also depletes the ozone layer.

Human activities have drastically increased GHG concentrations since the Industrial Revolution, intensifying climate impacts like rising temperatures, extreme weather, and sea-level rise. Mitigation strategies include transitioning to renewable energy, improving agricultural practices, and enhancing carbon sequestration. Immediate action is crucial to limit global temperature rise and avoid catastrophic climate effects.
