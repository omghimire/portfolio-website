Sea Level Rise and Coastal Erosion

Rising sea levels, driven by melting glaciers and thermal expansion of warming oceans, are accelerating coastal erosion worldwide. Since 1900, global sea levels have risen by 20 cm (8 inches), with the rate doubling since 2006 due to climate change. By 2100, projections suggest a 0.5 to 1-meter rise, threatening nearly 200 million people in low-lying coastal areas.

Coastal erosion worsens as higher sea levels intensify wave energy, wearing away shorelines and destroying protective barriers like mangroves, coral reefs, and dunes. Iconic coastlines, from Florida to Bangladesh, are losing 3 to 5 meters of land annually, displacing communities and damaging infrastructure. Small island nations, such as the Maldives and Tuvalu, face existential risks, with some islands already submerged. Saltwater intrusion also contaminates freshwater supplies and farmland, crippling local economies.

Mitigation requires reducing greenhouse gas emissions to slow ice melt, while adaptation strategies—such as restoring wetlands, building seawalls, and managed retreats—are critical to protect vulnerable regions. Without urgent action, rising seas will reshape coastlines, erase cultures, and trigger mass migrations, making this one of the most pressing challenges of our time. The window to act is closing fast.
